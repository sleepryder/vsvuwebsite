Description

The “Optical Error” is my bachelor’s thesis project, born from a deep sense of inquiry that has grown with me over the years. These questions, rooted in the formation of my identity, created significant voids - gaps that demanded filling but instead gave rise to internal contradictions. These discrepancies disrupted any sense of equilibrium and obscured my understanding of what I truly needed. My work began as an exploration of this paradox, an attempt to capture and decode its layers, while simultaneously navigating my place within it. At its core, this project examines internal conflicts, visualized through optical distortions and errors, with a particular focus on the phenomenon of identity blending and the persistent struggle of self-identification, especially in the context of voluntary migration.

This endeavor is shaped by personal history, which I weave into the realm of photographic art and visual media. My approach is further enriched by a familial legacy: my grandfathers were physicists who often mused about the blurred boundaries between truth and fiction in science. This inheritance fuels my fascination with wandering myths tied to the history of astronomy, as well as the power of montage to reframe reality. By incorporating these influences, I approach the questions of self-knowledge and identity not through generalized scientific observations but through a lens that probes the edges of perception. Using optical techniques, I engage in a dialogue between the physical and the metaphorical, exploring how distortions, whether of light or identity, can reveal hidden truths within constructed fictions.

Exhibition Description

Navigating the Unseen: A Reflection on Simulations

Throughout my creative journey, I have investigated the relationship between reality and simulation, authenticity and distortion, often through optical experiments and archival materials. These fragments now coalesce into a single narrative, where the viewer becomes both an observer and a participant. What fascinates me most is crafting environments that evoke the sensation of discovery, an almost scientific curiosity, while subtly nudging individuals toward introspection. The installation plays with notions of truth and perception, offering a space where the audience can question their interpretations and, perhaps, find resonance with their own internal narratives. By layering elements of optical science, pseudo-archival aesthetics, and interactive media, this project not only synthesizes my past explorations but also pushes me toward new creative territories.
